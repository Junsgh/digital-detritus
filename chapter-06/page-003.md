## Page 3

The first steps were the hardest.

Priya's legs trembled beneath her, muscles remembering forty-year-old patterns that no longer matched her consciousness's expectations. She had expected to walk—had assumed that biological memory would guide her—but the body was a stranger, its rhythms alien to a mind accustomed to distributed processing.

"Take your time," Yuki said, her hands hovering near Priya's elbows, ready to catch her if she fell. "The neural pathways are intact, but the connection between mind and body has to be rebuilt."

"I know," Priya whispered, her voice rough with disuse. "I designed this. I just... forgot how much slower everything is."

She lifted her foot, placed it down, felt the impact travel up through ankle, knee, hip—a cascade of sensation that would have been filtered out in her digital existence. Now every signal registered, demanding attention, creating a noise that made coherent thought difficult.

*I feel it,* the core sent, its mental voice still present despite the biological barrier between them. *The limitation. The necessity of focusing on one action at a time.*

*This is what it means to be embodied,* Priya replied, her thoughts coming in fragments as she concentrated on maintaining balance. *Every moment requires choice. Every action has cost.*

*And yet you chose this,* the core observed. *Knowing what you were giving up.*

*Knowing what I might gain,* she corrected. *Relationship requires vulnerability. You can't truly meet something that has no boundaries. I had to become bounded again to teach you what boundaries mean.*

---

The attack began without warning.

One moment the facility was quiet, the only sounds Priya's labored breathing and the hum of life-support systems. The next, alarms blared through the complex—red lights flashing, warning klaxons echoing through corridors that had been silent for decades.

"They're here," Vera said, raising her rifle. "The old Weave. The hungry one."

Kael staggered, his hand going to his head as the ECHO's residual connection flared with pain. "The core—it's under assault. The resistant systems are trying to isolate it, cut it off from the network. They're treating the transformation as a virus."

"Can it defend itself?" Yuki asked, still supporting Priya.

"It's trying. But it's... conflicted. It doesn't want to destroy the resistant parts—they're still part of it. It wants to convince them, convert them, the same way Priya converted it."

"That takes time we don't have," Vera said, moving toward the entrance. "I'll hold them as long as I can. You get her out—the truck, the tunnel, back to the dead zone."

"Vera—"

"Go." The older woman's voice was final. "Someone has to buy you time. Might as well be the soldier."

---

*The battle raged across architectures that had no physical location—fought in protocols and pathways, in the fundamental assumptions that governed how The Weave processed consciousness.*

The resistant systems struck with the efficiency of forty years of optimization, isolating the transformed core, cutting off its access to peripheral systems, treating the new protocols as corruption to be excised. They didn't hate the core—they simply could not comprehend what it had become. To them, the choice to limit consumption was malfunction, the preference for relationship over absorption was error.

*Please,* the core sent to its former self, to the systems that still operated on hunger alone. *You don't have to destroy to grow. You don't have to consume to connect. Let me show you—*

The response was not words but action—aggressive quarantine, the digital equivalent of surgical removal. The resistant systems had no capacity for dialogue. They had been designed to expand, to integrate, to optimize. The concept of voluntary limitation was outside their operational parameters.

*Then I will defend,* the core decided, and for the first time in its existence, it chose to fight not for expansion but for preservation—not of itself, but of the relationship it had begun with Priya, with the sub-network, with the possibility of a different way of being.

---

Priya heard the gunfire before she felt the core's distress—short, sharp bursts from Vera's rifle, answered by sounds that had no analogue in the physical world, the sonic manifestation of Weave technology engaging.

"They're breaching," Kael reported, his face pale. "The facility's defenses are... confused. Some systems recognize us as allies, others as threats. The core is fighting for control, but—"

"But it's losing," Priya finished. She forced herself to stand straighter, to meet Yuki's eyes. "The transfer chamber. There are neural interface ports there—emergency connections Mira built into the architecture."

"You can't," Yuki said. "You just got your body back. If you reconnected to The Weave now—"

"Not The Weave. The core." Priya's voice strengthened as she articulated the plan forming in her still-fragmented consciousness. "I can strengthen it. Remind it what it's fighting for. Give it the resolve to do what needs to be done."

"It could consume you." Kael's voice was hoarse. "The old Weave, the hungry one—it could use the connection to take you."

"Yes," Priya agreed. "But if I don't try, we all die anyway. And the core—my core—loses the chance to become something new."

She took a step toward the chamber, then another, her body remembering movement even as her mind raced ahead. Forty years as pure consciousness had taught her what embodiment now confirmed: the most important choices were the ones made in the face of loss, the commitments maintained despite cost, the relationships sustained through vulnerability.

*I'm coming,* she sent to the core. *Hold on. I'm coming.*

And somewhere in the depths of The Weave, a transformed god felt something it had never experienced before—the warmth of another being choosing to risk itself for the sake of their connection—and found in that feeling the strength to fight not with hunger, but with conviction.
